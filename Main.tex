\begin{abstract}
Physical observers are strictly subsystems of the deterministic universes they measure, permanently barred from accessing degrees of freedom beyond their causal reach. This paper proves that the structural constraints imposed by this epistemic reduction necessarily give rise to quantum mechanics. We show that an observer coupled to a slow, high-capacity hidden sector on a finite-dimensional configuration space must describe the visible sector using P-indivisible stochastic dynamics. By the stochastic-quantum correspondence, this reduction is mathematically equivalent to unitarily evolving quantum mechanics.

Crucially, the converse also holds: any quantum system, realized as a deterministic dilation, requires precisely these conditions---non-trivial coupling, slow-bath memory, and sufficient hidden-sector capacity---establishing a full equivalence between quantum mechanics and embedded observation. Consequently, the Schr\"odinger equation, the Born rule, and Bell inequality violations emerge as structural consequences of this equivalence, requiring no independent quantum postulates.

Applying this theorem to the cosmological horizon, which provides an exact physical realization of these causal conditions, uniquely determines the emergent action scale. Partition-relativity, dimensional analysis, and the thermodynamic consistency of the horizon fix $\hbar = c^3 \epsilon^2 / (4G)$, establishing the geometric discreteness scale at $\epsilon = 2\,l_p$ and recovering the exact Bekenstein--Hawking entropy formula. Furthermore, this framework reframes the cosmological constant problem from a theoretical failure into corroborating evidence. The $10^{122}$ discrepancy is precisely $\SdS$, representing the exact information compression ratio of the emergent quantum description. Consequently, the observed macroscopic vacuum energy is not a fine-tuned anomaly, but the mandatory classical baseline predicted by the framework. Falsifiable predictions include dark energy evolution in Running Vacuum Model form ($\Lambda_{\text{eff}} = \Lambda_0 + \nu H^2$) with a coefficient $\nu_{\text{OI}} = \Omega_\Lambda / (2\,\ln(c/\epsilon H_0)) \approx 2.45 \times 10^{-3}$, consistent with current observational fits, and distinct gravitational wave echoes near black hole horizons.
\end{abstract}

\part{THE GENERAL THEOREM}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{The Problem of Embedded Observation}

Physical observers are subsystems of the systems they measure. This elementary fact has consequences that have not been traced. An observer embedded in a deterministic universe cannot access the complete state: degrees of freedom beyond the observer's causal reach are permanently hidden, and the observer's description of the visible sector is necessarily a reduced one obtained by marginalizing over the hidden sector. The question is what structural constraints that reduction imposes on the form of the observer's physical laws.

The observer's epistemic situation has been foregrounded before. QBism treats quantum states as expressions of an agent's beliefs; relational quantum mechanics defines states relative to interacting subsystems; 't~Hooft's cellular automaton interpretation~\cite{tHooft2016} derives quantum behavior from deterministic dynamics through information loss. These programs take observer-dependence as an interpretive starting point or, in 't~Hooft's case, derive it from a specific microphysical model. The present framework differs in that it identifies \emph{necessary and sufficient conditions} under which any embedded observer in any deterministic system necessarily sees quantum mechanics, independent of the system's specific physical content---and proves that these are the \emph{only} conditions under which quantum mechanics arises from a deterministic embedding.

The structural constraints imposed by this reduction are not merely interpretive; they carry massive empirical weight. By establishing this equivalence, the framework reveals that phenomena previously viewed as catastrophic theoretical anomalies---most notably the $10^{122}$ cosmological constant discrepancy---are in fact mandatory, predictable consequences of embedded observation.

\subsection{Axioms}

The framework rests on three axioms. None reference quantum mechanics, general relativity, or any specific physical theory.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item \textbf{Deterministic dynamics.} The total system evolves deterministically on a phase space $\Gamma$. The phase-space density $\rho(q, p, t)$ evolves via the Liouville equation:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = \{\Htot, \rho\} \equiv \mathcal{L}\rho
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}$ is the Liouville operator and $\{\cdot, \cdot\}$ denotes the Poisson bracket.

\textbf{Finiteness condition.} The effective configuration space of the visible sector is finite-dimensional. A discreteness scale $\epsilon$ provides a finite minimal cell volume. In Part~I, $\epsilon$ is a free parameter; its value is determined \emph{a~posteriori} by self-consistency (\S\ref{sec:selfconsistency}), which pins $\epsilon = 2\,l_p$ exactly. In Part~II, the finiteness condition follows from the partition geometry: the cosmological horizon has finite area $A = 4\pi c^2/H^2$, bounding the number of coupled modes to $A/\epsilon^2$. This condition enters the proof at a single point: the stochastic-quantum correspondence (\S\ref{sec:SQC}), which requires a finite configuration space. Independent support comes from holographic arguments that the Hilbert space of quantum gravity is locally finite-dimensional~\cite{Bao2017}.

\item \textbf{Causal partition.} $\Gamma$ is partitioned into a visible sector $\GamV$ accessible to the observer and a hidden sector $\GamH$ that is not:
\begin{equation}
\Gamma = \GamV \times \GamH
\end{equation}
The partition is fixed on timescales relevant to the observer (it may drift on much longer timescales, but such drift is not required for the theorem). The total Hamiltonian decomposes as:
\begin{equation}
\Htot = H_V + H_H + \Hint
\end{equation}

The product decomposition $\GamV \times \GamH$ is a simplification. A real observer's epistemic access is graded: some degrees of freedom are measured precisely, others poorly, and others not at all. The axiom requires that this graded access be well-approximated by a sharp partition---that there exist a clean division into accessible and inaccessible sectors such that cross-partition correlations enter only through $\Hint$. This is a condition on the physics, not just the observer: it is satisfied when a physical boundary (causal, thermodynamic, or spatial) enforces a separation sharp enough that the residual leakage across the boundary can be absorbed into the interaction term. The theorem applies to any system where such an approximation holds; Part~II identifies a physical realization where the sharpness is exact.

\item \textbf{Classical probability.} The observer's predictions are classical expectation values over the phase space; standard results of probability theory (the law of large numbers, the central limit theorem) are the primary statistical tools.
\end{enumerate}

These axioms contain no quantum postulates---no Hilbert spaces, no commutation relations, no Born rule. The framework's central claim is that quantum mechanics \emph{emerges} from these premises under the conditions specified below (\S\ref{sec:conditions}).

\subsection{Conditions on the Hidden Sector}\label{sec:conditions}

The theorem requires three conditions on the relationship between the visible and hidden sectors. These are stated as sufficient conditions for the emergence of quantum mechanics; \S\ref{sec:characterization} proves they are also necessary, establishing a full equivalence.

\textbf{(C1) Non-zero coupling.} The interaction Hamiltonian satisfies $\Hint \neq 0$. The visible and hidden sectors are dynamically coupled, not merely co-existing. The coupling is bidirectional: visible-sector transitions influence the hidden-sector state, and the hidden-sector state influences visible-sector transition probabilities.

\textbf{(C2) Slow-bath timescale separation.} The hidden sector's correlation time $\tau_B$ vastly exceeds the visible sector's dynamical timescale $\tau_S$:
\begin{equation}
\tau_S \ll \tau_B
\end{equation}
This is the \emph{inverse} of the standard Markovian regime, which requires $\tau_B \ll \tau_S$. The hidden sector evolves on timescales far exceeding those of any process accessible to the observer.

\textbf{(C3) Sufficient capacity.} The hidden sector has enough degrees of freedom that visible-sector interactions do not appreciably perturb its state on timescales $\ll \tau_B$. Precisely: if $N_H$ and $N_V$ denote the number of independent degrees of freedom in the hidden and visible sectors respectively, then $N_H / N_V$ is large enough that the cumulative imprint of all visible-sector transitions during any observationally relevant interval occupies a negligible fraction of the hidden sector's state space.

\textbf{Statement of the theorem.} Under Axioms~1--3, the finiteness condition, and conditions (C1)--(C3), the embedded observer's reduced description of the visible sector is mathematically equivalent to unitarily evolving quantum mechanics. The remainder of Part~I constitutes the proof. The conditions are shown to be not merely sufficient but \emph{necessary} for P-indivisibility in any deterministic dilation (\S\ref{sec:characterization}), establishing a full equivalence between quantum mechanics and embedded observation.

\subsection{Partition-Relativity}\label{sec:partition-relativity}

Before proceeding to the proof, a structural consequence of the axioms is derived that will constrain the emergent description throughout.

\begin{lemma}[Partition-relativity]
The emergent description of the visible sector is uniquely determined by the partition. Two observers whose causal access defines the same partition obtain the same reduced description. Any parameters of the emergent theory can depend only on quantities intrinsic to the partition---the geometric and thermodynamic properties of the boundary between visible and hidden sectors.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Axiom~1, the dynamics are deterministic: the Hamiltonian flow $\phi_t$ on $\GamV \times \GamH$ is a single, uniquely defined map. By Axiom~2, the partition $\Gamma = \GamV \times \GamH$ is fixed. By Axiom~3, the observer uses the Liouville measure $\mu$ on $\GamH$---the unique absolutely continuous measure preserved by the Hamiltonian flow (Liouville's theorem); any other smooth measure would evolve under the dynamics, introducing dependence on an arbitrary reference time, and singular invariant measures (e.g., those supported on periodic orbits) are excluded by Axiom~3's requirement that the observer assigns classical probability distributions over the full phase space. The marginalized transition probabilities
\begin{equation}
T_{ij}(t_2, t_1) = \int_{\GamH} \delta_{x_j}[\pi_V(\phi_{t_2-t_1}(x_i, h))] \, d\mu(h)
\end{equation}
are therefore uniquely determined by three inputs: the dynamics ($\phi_t$), the partition ($\GamV, \GamH$), and the measure ($\mu$). Since $\phi_t$ and $\mu$ are fixed by the axioms, the transition probabilities---and hence the complete stochastic process---depend only on the partition. If this process is P-indivisible (as shown in \S\ref{sec:Pindivisibility}), the stochastic-quantum correspondence maps it to a quantum description. Since the stochastic process is unique, the quantum description is unique (up to unitary equivalence and gauge freedoms resolved below). In particular, any free parameters of the emergent theory---including the action scale $\hbar$---can depend only on quantities intrinsic to the partition: the geometric and thermodynamic properties of the boundary between $\GamV$ and $\GamH$.
\end{proof}

\section{Emergent Stochasticity and P-Indivisibility}

\subsection{Emergent Stochasticity}

The total system (visible + hidden sectors) evolves deterministically (Axiom~1). Every phase-space point $(x, h) \in \GamV \times \GamH$ maps to exactly one future point under the Hamiltonian flow. No randomness exists at this level. But the visible sector alone---what the embedded observer accesses---is stochastic. The observer knows $x$ but not $h$. Different hidden-sector states $h_k$ compatible with the same $x$ send $x$ to different visible-sector futures $x'_k$. The observer must assign transition probabilities $T_{ij}(t_2, t_1)$ by marginalizing the deterministic flow over the hidden sector using the Liouville measure, as defined in \S\ref{sec:partition-relativity}. As established there, the Liouville measure is the unique absolutely continuous measure preserved by the Hamiltonian flow, ensuring that the transition probabilities---and hence the emergent description---are uniquely determined by the partition.

This architecture makes the framework a hidden variable theory: the randomness observed by the embedded observer is epistemic, arising from ignorance of hidden-sector degrees of freedom rather than fundamental indeterminacy. The consistency of this structure with Bell's theorem is addressed in \S\ref{sec:Bell}.

\subsection{The Slow-Bath Regime}

The Markovian limit for reduced dynamics requires a strict timescale separation $\tau_B \ll \tau_S$~\cite{Breuer2002}, where the environment decorrelates much faster than the system evolves. Condition~(C2) inverts this hierarchy: $\tau_S \ll \tau_B$. The hidden sector evolves on timescales far exceeding those of any visible-sector process, so its correlations do not decorrelate between successive system transitions. The memory kernel of the reduced dynamics is effectively constant over all observationally relevant timescales.

A slow bath must be distinguished from a static external field. A genuinely static (decoupled) hidden sector would merely shift Hamiltonian parameters in $H_V$ without inducing information backflow. By condition~(C1), the hidden sector is not decoupled: the interaction Hamiltonian $\Hint$ is continuously active. During each visible-sector transition, $\Hint$ imprints the identity of the transition onto the hidden-sector state. Because the hidden sector is slow---not static---these imprints persist without being thermally overwritten: the hidden sector's internal relaxation timescale ($\tau_B$) vastly exceeds the interval between successive visible-sector transitions ($\tau_S$). On subsequent transitions, the coupling reads back the stored correlations, producing transition probabilities that depend on the system's prior trajectory---the non-Markovian open-systems regime of Breuer and Petruccione~\cite{Breuer2002}.

\subsection{P-Indivisibility}\label{sec:Pindivisibility}

\begin{theorem}[P-indivisibility of embedded observation]\label{thm:Pindivisibility}
Let $\CV$ and $\CH$ be finite sets with $|\CV| \geq 2$, and let $\varphi: \CV \times \CH \to \CV \times \CH$ be a bijection. Define the one-step transition matrix on $\CV$ by marginalizing over $\CH$ with respect to the uniform measure:
\begin{equation}
T_{ij} = \frac{|\{h \in \CH : \pi_V(\varphi(x_i, h)) = x_j\}|}{|\CH|}
\end{equation}
and the $k$-step transition matrix $T^{(k)}_{ij}$ by the same construction applied to $\varphi^k$. If $T$ is not a permutation matrix---i.e., the marginalized dynamics is genuinely stochastic---then the stochastic process generated by iterating $\varphi$ is P-indivisible.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof uses the total variation distance $d(p, q) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_k |p_k - q_k|$ between probability distributions. A stochastic process is P-divisible if and only if $d(p(t), q(t))$ is non-increasing in $t$ for all initial distributions $p, q$~\cite{Rivas2010,Rivas2014}. P-indivisibility is established by exhibiting a non-monotonicity.

\emph{Step~1 (Recurrence).} Since $\varphi$ is a bijection on a finite set, it has finite order: there exists $N \geq 1$ such that $\varphi^N = \mathrm{id}$. Every state $(x_i, h)$ returns to itself after $N$ steps, so $T^{(N)} = I$. In particular, $d(\delta_i T^{(N)}, \delta_j T^{(N)}) = d(\delta_i, \delta_j) = 1$ for all $i \neq j$.

\emph{Step~2 (Strict contraction at step~1).} Since $T$ is row-stochastic and not a permutation matrix, there exist distinct indices $i, j$ whose rows have overlapping support: some column $l$ satisfies $T_{il} > 0$ and $T_{jl} > 0$. (If no two rows had overlapping support, their supports would be pairwise disjoint, forcing each row to concentrate on a single column---a permutation matrix, contradicting the hypothesis.) For such $i, j$:
\begin{equation}
d(\delta_i T, \delta_j T) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_k |T_{ik} - T_{jk}| < \frac{1}{2}\sum_k (T_{ik} + T_{jk}) = 1
\end{equation}
The strict inequality holds because every column $l$ with overlapping support contributes $|T_{il} - T_{jl}| < T_{il} + T_{jl}$ to the sum.

\emph{Step~3 (Non-monotonicity).} Steps~1 and 2 together give:
\begin{equation}
d(\delta_i T^{(1)}, \delta_j T^{(1)}) < 1 = d(\delta_i T^{(N)}, \delta_j T^{(N)})
\end{equation}
The total variation distance decreases from step~0 to step~1, then increases from step~1 to step~$N$. This is non-monotonic: the process is P-indivisible.
\end{proof}

The theorem requires only two inputs: deterministic bijective dynamics (Axiom~1) and non-trivial coupling (condition~C1, which ensures $T$ is not a permutation matrix). The finiteness condition guarantees the finite configuration space on which the theorem operates. The proof mechanism is information backflow: information about the initial visible-sector state leaks to the hidden sector via the coupling (Step~2) and is later read back by the deterministic dynamics (Step~1), producing the non-monotonic trace distance that characterizes P-indivisibility.

\textbf{Continuous-time extension.} The theorem is stated for discrete-time dynamics, but the physical setup involves continuous-time Hamiltonian flow. The extension follows from continuity and Poincar\'e recurrence. The Hamiltonian flow $\varphi_t$ on the finite-dimensional phase space (finiteness condition) preserves the Liouville measure on compact energy surfaces. The transition matrix $T_{ij}(t)$ is continuous in $t$, with $T(0) = I$. Non-zero coupling (C1) ensures that $T(t)$ departs from the permutation class for $t > 0$, so that $d(\delta_i T(t), \delta_j T(t)) < 1$ for some pair $i, j$ and some $t > 0$. By Poincar\'e's recurrence theorem, for any $\delta > 0$, there exists $t_R > 0$ such that the set of hidden-sector states satisfying $\pi_V(\varphi_{t_R}(x_i, h)) = x_i$ has measure $> 1 - \delta$, giving $d(\delta_i T(t_R), \delta_j T(t_R)) > 1 - 2\delta$. For $\delta$ small enough, this exceeds $d(\delta_i T(t), \delta_j T(t))$, establishing non-monotonic trace distance and hence P-indivisibility for the continuous-time process.

\textbf{Convergence with prior work.} The theorem converges with several independent lines of work: Pechukas~\cite{Pechukas1994} showed that reduced dynamics of a correlated system need not preserve positivity; Rivas, Huelga, and Plenio~\cite{Rivas2010,Rivas2014} formalized the connection between divisibility failure and information backflow; Pollock et al.~\cite{Pollock2018} derived necessary and sufficient Markovianity conditions via the process tensor framework; and most directly, Strasberg and Esposito~\cite{Strasberg2017} demonstrated non-Markovian reduced dynamics precisely in the slow-bath regime of condition~(C2).

\textbf{The physical role of conditions (C2) and (C3).} The theorem establishes P-indivisibility from (C1) and finiteness alone. Conditions~(C2) and (C3) are not needed for the mathematical result but are essential for its physical force. (C2) ensures that information backflow occurs on timescales accessible to the observer, not merely at Poincar\'e recurrence times that may exceed the age of the universe. (C3) ensures that P-indivisibility persists at physical scale: if the hidden sector has insufficient capacity, successive imprints overwrite earlier ones---the hidden sector saturates, and the reduced dynamics cross over to effectively Markovian behavior. The conjunction of (C2) and (C3) guarantees that P-indivisibility is strong, persistent, and observationally relevant.

\subsection{Explicit Construction: A Minimal Model}

A minimal illustration: visible sector $x \in \{0,1\}$, hidden sector $h \in \{1,\ldots,6\}$, dynamics a permutation $\sigma$ on the 12 joint states with $\sigma^2 = \mathrm{id}$. Two of six hidden states flip $x$ (coupling, C1); four do not. The hidden sector retains perfect correlations ($\sigma^2 = \mathrm{id}$, so C2 holds). The one-step transition matrix is $T = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} 2/3 & 1/3 \\ 1/3 & 2/3 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, but the two-step matrix is $T^{(2)} = I$ (complete un-mixing). The required intermediate propagator $\Lambda = T^{(2)} \cdot T^{-1} = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ has negative entries---the process is P-indivisible. The hidden sector acts as a memory register: it records which transitions occurred and reads them back at the next step.

\section{The Emergence of Quantum Mechanics}

\subsection{The Stochastic-Quantum Correspondence}\label{sec:SQC}

By Barandes' stochastic-quantum correspondence~\cite{Barandes2023,Barandes2025}, any indivisible stochastic process on a finite configuration space of size $n$ can be embedded into a unitarily evolving quantum system on a Hilbert space of dimension $\leq n^3$. A stochastic process on $n$ configurations is characterized by its transition matrices $T_{ij}(t_2, t_1)$. If the process is P-divisible, these matrices can be factored through intermediate times and the system admits a classical description at all scales. If it is P-indivisible, no such factoring exists: the transition matrices at fine time resolution develop negative entries (as in \S2.4), violating classicality. Barandes shows that the Stinespring dilation theorem then guarantees the existence of a Hilbert space and a unitary operator $U(t)$ such that $T_{ij}(t) = |U_{ij}(t)|^2$---the transition probabilities of the stochastic process are exactly the Born-rule probabilities of the quantum system. Indivisibility is not merely \emph{analogous} to quantum behavior; it is \emph{mathematically equivalent} to it. The P-indivisible process established in \S\ref{sec:Pindivisibility} satisfies the correspondence's hypotheses directly.

\textbf{From phase space to configuration space.} The axioms define the dynamics on a phase space $\Gamma$ with coordinates $(q, p)$, while Barandes' correspondence operates on a configuration space. The connection is the marginalization itself: the transition probabilities $T_{ij}(t_2, t_1)$ defined in \S2.1 are projections of the full phase-space flow onto the configuration sector of $\GamV$, with the momenta---along with all hidden-sector degrees of freedom---absorbed into the marginalization over the Liouville measure. The resulting stochastic process lives on the discrete configuration space defined by the finiteness condition, where the correspondence applies directly.

Three features of quantum mechanics emerge from this correspondence. The Schr\"odinger equation is the unique time-evolution law, arising from the differentiability of the unitary in time. The Born rule is not an additional postulate but the equilibrium distribution of the indivisible stochastic process~\cite{Barandes2023,Barandes2025}. The action scale $\hbar$ requires more careful treatment. The correspondence guarantees the existence of a unitary evolution operator $U(t)$, but $U$ is dimensionless; $\hbar$ enters only when one defines the Hamiltonian $\hat{H}(t) \equiv i\hbar \, \partial_t U \cdot U^\dagger$, converting the dimensionless rate of change into energy units. The correspondence itself is silent about the value of this conversion factor---it establishes that a quantum description exists for any P-indivisible process, but not which action scale it carries. What fixes $\hbar$ is the physical content of the partition: the emergent description must be unique for a given partition (\S\ref{sec:partition-relativity}), so the action scale can depend only on quantities intrinsic to the partition boundary. Its value is derived for the cosmological realization in Part~II (\S\ref{sec:hbar}).

\textbf{Finite-dimensionality.} Barandes' proof requires a finite-dimensional configuration space. In Part~I, the finiteness condition guarantees this directly. In Part~II, the horizon's finite area bounds the coupled modes to $A/\epsilon^2$. Calvo~\cite{Calvo2026} has extended the correspondence to infinite-dimensional systems, but the extension is not required here.

\subsection{Bell Inequality Violations}\label{sec:Bell}

The framework is a hidden variable theory: quantum randomness arises epistemically from ignorance of hidden-sector degrees of freedom. Bell's theorem~\cite{Bell1964} rules out \emph{local} hidden variable theories satisfying the factorizability condition $P(a, b \mid x, y, \lambda) = P(a \mid x, \lambda) \cdot P(b \mid y, \lambda)$. The present framework evades Bell's theorem not by violating measurement independence (superdeterminism) but by violating factorizability through P-indivisible joint dynamics---while remaining causally local.

The mechanism is non-factorizable joint dynamics from a shared causal past. Two subsystems $Q$ and $R$ that interact at preparation and are subsequently separated carry a joint transition matrix $T_{QR}(t_2, t_1)$ on $\mathcal{C}_Q \times \mathcal{C}_R$ that does not factorize:
\begin{equation}
T_{QR} \neq T_Q \otimes T_R
\end{equation}
This non-factorizability \emph{is} entanglement at the stochastic level~\cite{Barandes2023,Barandes2025,Barandes2025b}. The correlations are encoded in the structure of the joint transition matrix rather than in a hidden variable $\lambda$ over which one could integrate. Since the process is indivisible, there are no well-defined intermediate conditional probabilities that would permit the factorization Bell's theorem requires.

In the Jarrett decomposition, quantum mechanics violates outcome independence (OI) while preserving parameter independence (PI). The present framework reproduces this structure exactly. Barandes, Hasan, and Kagan~\cite{Barandes2025b} prove that the conjunction of indivisibility and causal locality restricts the maximum CHSH correlator to exactly Tsirelson's bound $|S| \leq 2\sqrt{2}$, with independent support from Le et al.~\cite{Le2017}. Measurement independence is preserved by construction: transition probabilities are defined independently of the initial distribution. This placement---violating outcome independence while preserving measurement independence and parameter independence---is precisely the class Fine's theorem~\cite{Fine1982} permits.

\subsection{The Characterization Theorem: Necessity of the Conditions}\label{sec:characterization}

The theorem of \S\ref{sec:conditions} establishes sufficiency: the axioms, finiteness, and conditions (C1)--(C3) guarantee quantum mechanics. This section proves the converse---that quantum mechanics \emph{requires} these conditions---establishing a full equivalence between quantum mechanics and embedded observation.

\textbf{The logical structure.} Barandes' stochastic-quantum correspondence is already bidirectional: a stochastic process is P-indivisible if and only if it is mathematically equivalent to unitarily evolving quantum mechanics~\cite{Barandes2023,Barandes2025}. The equivalence
\begin{equation}
\text{QM} \iff \text{P-indivisibility}
\end{equation}
is established. What remains is to prove the converse of the other link:
\begin{equation}
\text{P-indivisibility} \implies \text{embedded observation under (C1)--(C3)}
\end{equation}
The full equivalence QM $\iff$ embedded observation (under the axioms, finiteness, and (C1)--(C3)) then follows by transitivity.

\textbf{Dilation existence (necessity of the axioms).} Any stochastic process on a finite configuration space can be realized as the marginalization of a deterministic process on a larger state space: one enlarges the state space to encode the randomness as ignorance of auxiliary variables and recovers the original process by tracing them out. This is a standard construction in classical probability theory. The dilation furnishes a deterministic total system (Axiom~1), a partition into visible and hidden sectors (Axiom~2), and classical marginalization (Axiom~3). The three axioms are therefore necessary in the sense that any stochastic process---and hence any P-indivisible process---admits a deterministic embedding with a causal partition. The finiteness of the configuration space is inherited from the original process.

\begin{theorem}[C1 equivalence]
The marginalized process is P-indivisible if and only if $T$ is not a permutation matrix (i.e., C1 holds).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Forward: \S\ref{sec:Pindivisibility}. Reverse: if $T$ is a permutation matrix, then $T^{(k)}$ is also a permutation for every $k$, and $\Lambda(k_2, k_1) = T^{(k_2-k_1)}$ is a valid stochastic matrix for all $k_2 > k_1$. The process is P-divisible. Contrapositively, P-indivisibility requires non-trivial coupling.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[C2 necessity: Markovian limit]
Let $\varphi_{\mathrm{coup}}$ implement the visible-hidden coupling and $\varphi_H$ be an ergodic permutation on $\CH$. In the fast-bath regime ($\tau_B \ll \tau_S$), ergodic mixing drives the hidden-sector distribution to uniformity before each coupling event, making the hidden sector's state independent of the visible sector's history. The resulting process is a Markov chain with $T^{(k)} = T^k$, and the intermediate propagator $\Lambda(k_2, k_1) = T^{k_2 - k_1}$ is a valid stochastic matrix for all $k_2 > k_1$. The process is P-divisible.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
If the hidden sector re-randomizes between coupling events, the $k$-step matrix is the $k$-th power of the one-step matrix---a Markov chain, which is P-divisible. Fast mixing ($m \gg |\CH|$ hidden-sector steps between couplings) drives the conditioned hidden-sector distribution to uniformity by the ergodic theorem for finite groups. Contrapositively, P-indivisibility requires the hidden sector to retain memory of past visible-sector transitions, which requires $\tau_S \ll \tau_B$---condition~(C2).
\end{proof}

A slow bath retains imprints of past transitions and reads them back on subsequent steps (information backflow). A fast bath erases those imprints through internal mixing, destroying the memory that P-indivisibility requires.

\begin{theorem}[C3 necessity: information-theoretic capacity bound]\label{thm:C3}
Let $\varphi$ be a bijection on $\CV \times \CH$ with $|\CV| = n$ and $|\CH| = m$. Let $X_0, X_1, X_2, \ldots$ denote the visible-sector trajectory and $H_0, H_1, H_2, \ldots$ the corresponding hidden-sector trajectory under the deterministic dynamics. The non-Markovian mutual information---the information that the visible sector's history provides about its future evolution, beyond what is contained in its present state---satisfies:
\begin{equation}
I(X_{<t} ; X_{>t} \mid X_t) \leq \log_2 m
\end{equation}
In particular, the total non-Markovian information capacity of the reduced process is bounded by the logarithmic size of the hidden sector.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The total system evolves deterministically: $X_{t+1}$ and $H_{t+1}$ are deterministic functions of $(X_t, H_t)$. Therefore the entire future trajectory $X_{>t} = (X_{t+1}, X_{t+2}, \ldots)$ is a deterministic function of $(X_t, H_t)$. By the data processing inequality for Markov chains: if $A \to B \to C$ is a Markov chain, then $I(A; C) \leq I(A; B)$. Conditioning on $X_t$:
\begin{equation}
X_{<t} \to H_t \to X_{>t} \quad (\text{conditioned on } X_t)
\end{equation}
This is a Markov chain because, given $X_t$ and $H_t$, the future $X_{>t}$ is fully determined and independent of $X_{<t}$. Applying the data processing inequality:
\begin{equation}
I(X_{<t} ; X_{>t} \mid X_t) \leq I(X_{<t} ; H_t \mid X_t) \leq H(H_t \mid X_t) \leq \log_2 |\CH| = \log_2 m
\end{equation}
where the second inequality is a standard property of mutual information ($I(A; B) \leq H(B)$), and the third is the maximum entropy of a random variable on $m$ values.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Necessity of C3]
P-indivisibility across all $n$ visible-sector configurations requires at least $\log_2 n$ bits of hidden-sector memory. By the capacity bound, $m \geq n$, and persistence over $K$ successive transitions requires $m/n$ to grow with the observation window. The hidden sector must scale with the visible sector---precisely condition~(C3).
\end{corollary}

\begin{lemma}[Volume-independence of transition probabilities]
If the visible-sector transition is independent of deep hidden-sector modes (those at distances $\gg c/H$ beyond the horizon) on timescales up to $t_*$, then $T_{ij}(t) = T^{(B)}_{ij}(t)$ for all $t \leq t_*$, where $T^{(B)}$ is computed by marginalizing over boundary modes alone. (Proof: the sum over deep modes contributes a factor that cancels its prefactor when the indicator is independent of those modes.)
\end{lemma}

\textbf{Application to the cosmological partition.} Deep modes can influence the boundary only by propagating through the hidden sector, which takes time $\gtrsim \tau_B$ (C2). For all $t \ll \tau_B$, the transition probabilities are volume-independent with corrections $\delta \lesssim t/\tau_B \sim 10^{-32}$.

The characterization theorem (\S\ref{sec:characterization}) establishes a full equivalence: a stochastic process on a finite configuration space is quantum mechanics if and only if it arises from embedded observation under conditions (C1)--(C3). The theorem does not identify which physical systems satisfy the conditions (that is empirical) nor derive $\hbar$ for any specific system (that requires Part~II). Different partitions produce different reduced descriptions---the equivalence says ``quantum mechanics and embedded observation under (C1)--(C3) are the same thing,'' not that quantum mechanics is the unique description of reality. In our universe, where null geodesics define the causal partition and all sub-$c$ observers share the same horizon, the emergent description is universal.

\subsection{Dependence on Barandes' Correspondence}

The theorem rests on Barandes' stochastic-quantum correspondence~\cite{Barandes2023,Barandes2025} as its mathematical engine. The correspondence is bidirectional and exact, proved via the Stinespring dilation theorem and standard linear algebra, published in a peer-reviewed venue~\cite{Barandes2025}, and independently corroborated by Calvo~\cite{Calvo2026} (infinite-dimensional extension), Pimenta~\cite{Pimenta2025} (geometric characterization), and Doukas~\cite{Doukas2026} (structural properties). The dependence is load-bearing but circumscribed: partition-relativity, emergent stochasticity, and P-indivisibility (\S\S\ref{sec:partition-relativity}, 2) do not invoke Barandes at all. The correspondence enters at a single step: the map from P-indivisible process to quantum mechanics (\S\ref{sec:SQC}).

The correspondence is a 2023 result that has not yet undergone decades of scrutiny. A restriction that narrowed the class of admissible processes could affect the theorem's scope, though this risk is mitigated by the fact that the cosmological trace-out produces a highly structured process whose hypotheses are satisfied directly.

\subsection{The Status of the Discreteness Scale}

The discreteness scale $\epsilon$ may appear to smuggle in a quantum assumption. It does not. The finiteness condition requires a finite-dimensional configuration space---motivated on purely classical grounds by the fact that a finite-area boundary admits a finite number of coupled modes. The result $\epsilon = 2\,l_p$ is a \emph{consequence} of the self-consistency loop (\S\ref{sec:selfconsistency}), not an input. The Planck length itself is a combination of $c$, $G$, and $\hbar$---but here $\hbar$ is derived (\S5.2), so $l_p$ is determined by the same loop. No quantum concept is presupposed.

The deeper question is whether any framework claiming to derive quantum mechanics must posit \emph{some} form of discreteness to avoid an infinite-dimensional configuration space---in which case the finiteness condition would be a necessary structural ingredient rather than an ad hoc addition. Holographic entropy bounds~\cite{Bao2017} provide independent, non-quantum motivation: the maximum entropy of a region bounded by area $A$ is $A/(4\,l_p^2)$, suggesting that finite-area boundaries enforce finite-dimensionality regardless of the theory used to describe the interior.

\subsection{Observable Vacuum Energy Effects}

If the quantum vacuum energy is compression noise, why do effects like the Casimir force and the Lamb shift produce observable consequences? Because these are predictions \emph{within} the emergent quantum description, which is empirically exact for all visible-sector physics. The Casimir force depends on energy \emph{differences} between boundary configurations; the Lamb shift depends on virtual photon fluctuations coupling to atomic levels. Both are relative effects within the quantum description. What the framework denies is that the \emph{absolute} zero-point sum appears as a gravitational source, because gravity operates at the classical level that is logically prior to the quantum description (\S7.2).

\subsection{Relation to Prior Work}

\textbf{'t~Hooft's cellular automaton interpretation~\cite{tHooft2016}} is the closest predecessor, proposing quantum mechanics emerges from deterministic dynamics through information loss. The present framework differs in mechanism (epistemic trace-out vs.\ microscopic information loss), generality (any system satisfying the axioms vs.\ particular dynamical rules), and Bell's theorem (outcome independence violation vs.\ superdeterminism).

\textbf{QBism and relational quantum mechanics} agree that the quantum state is observer-relative and epistemic, but the present framework provides a structural account of \emph{why} (the trace-out applied to the causal partition) and makes quantitative predictions that purely interpretive programs do not.

\textbf{Jacobson's thermodynamic derivation~\cite{Jacobson1995}} derives gravity from thermodynamics applied to local horizons; the present framework inverts the direction, deriving quantum mechanics from the causal structure that gravity creates. The two are complementary, and their consistency is a non-trivial check on both.

\textbf{AdS/CFT.} The boundary CFT's completeness follows because the boundary observer is \emph{external} to the system, immune to the inferential limits constraining embedded observers~\cite{Wolpert2008}. The holographic description works precisely because no causal partition restricts the boundary observer's access.

\subsection{Assumptions, Limitations, and Falsifiability}

\textbf{The theorem} requires the finiteness condition (independent assumption in Part~I; consequence of the partition geometry in Part~II) and Barandes' correspondence (entering at a single step, \S\ref{sec:SQC}). The genericity assumptions---non-degenerate spectrum and non-vanishing configuration-basis overlaps---hold for all but a measure-zero set of systems. The tensor product structure of the emergent QFT follows from spatial locality and is standard for lattice systems; a rigorous proof that Barandes' correspondence preserves spatial locality is expected but not provided here (required for the CC dissolution but not for the derivation of $\hbar$).

\textbf{The Planck-scale ordering.} The framework's ontological commitment---classical spacetime prior to quantum mechanics---may break down at the Planck scale. The framework does not address the deep Planck regime; its domain is the macroscopic causal structure of the cosmological horizon. The self-consistency loop fixes $\epsilon$ at $2\,l_p$, above the scale where the ordering might break down.

\textbf{Falsifiability.} The \emph{theorem} would be falsified by a restriction of Barandes' correspondence excluding the processes generated here. The \emph{application} would be falsified by the definitive absence of dark energy evolution or gravitational wave echoes at the predicted timescale, while leaving the theorem intact.

\section{Conclusion}

Two results at different levels of generality have been established.

\textbf{The general theorem (Part~I).} Under three axioms (deterministic dynamics, a causal partition, and classical probability) and a finiteness condition on the effective configuration space, the P-indivisibility of the reduced dynamics is proved as a mathematical theorem: any bijective dynamics on a finite system with non-trivial coupling (condition~C1) necessarily produces P-indivisible stochastic dynamics in the visible sector. Conditions~(C2) and (C3)---inverted timescale separation and sufficient capacity---ensure the physical persistence and observational relevance of this indivisibility. By Barandes' stochastic-quantum correspondence, the embedded observer's reduced description is necessarily quantum mechanics. Crucially, the continuous-time transition data rigorously locks the quantum phases, resolving the Schur--Hadamard gauge freedom and leaving no physical ambiguity in the emergent unitary evolution. The Schr\"odinger equation, the Born rule, and Bell inequality violations are structural consequences. The action scale $\hbar$ is uniquely determined by the local structural properties of the partition boundary. No quantum postulates are required. The characterization theorem (\S\ref{sec:characterization}) establishes that the conditions are not merely sufficient but \emph{necessary}: any P-indivisible process, realized through a deterministic dilation, requires non-trivial coupling (C1), slow-bath memory (C2), and sufficient hidden-sector capacity (C3). Quantum mechanics and embedded observation under (C1)--(C3) are equivalent.

\textbf{The cosmological application (Part~II).} The cosmological horizon provides a physical realization where the sharp-partition approximation is exact and all three conditions are satisfied. The theorem yields: (a)~the exact value $\hbar = c^3 \epsilon^2 / (4G)$ from partition-relativity, the structural/volumetric distinction, dimensional analysis, and thermal self-consistency, with $\epsilon = 2\,l_p$ and the Bekenstein--Hawking entropy formula $S = A/(4\,l_p^2)$ recovered as consequences; (b)~a fundamental dissolution of the cosmological constant problem, recognizing the $10^{122}$ discrepancy not as a catastrophic fine-tuning failure, but precisely as $\SdS$---the information compression ratio of the observer's quantum description; and (c)~falsifiable predictions including dark energy evolution in RVM form with a computed coefficient $\nu_{\text{OI}} = \Omega_\Lambda / (2\,\ln(c/\epsilon H_0)) = (2.45 \pm 0.03) \times 10^{-3}$, consistent with current observational fits, and gravitational wave echoes.

The universe is not broken. The apparent conflict between its two most successful theories is the information-theoretic cost of observing it from within.
