\title{A Parameter-Free Prediction of the Solar Mixing Angle\\
from Cubic-Group Flavor Structure, Tested by JUNO}

\author{Alex Maybaum}
\email{[email]}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
The first JUNO measurement of reactor neutrino oscillations
reports $\sin^2\theta_{12} = 0.3092 \pm 0.0087$, with the
post-JUNO global fit tightening this to
$\sin^2\theta_{12} = 0.3085 \pm 0.0073$. We show that
treating the cubic point group $O$ — the symmetry group of
three-dimensional space — as the underlying symmetry of the
lepton sector yields the prediction
$\sin^2\theta_{12} = \tfrac{1}{3} - \tfrac{1}{4\pi^2} = 0.3080$,
matching the global fit at $0.07\sigma$, with no parameter fit
to the value of $\sin^2\theta_{12}$. The same construction
gives $\sin^2\theta_{13} = 4/(18\pi^2) = 0.02252$ and
$\sin^2\theta_{23} = \tfrac{1}{2} + 1/(2\pi^2) = 0.5507$,
each consistent with NuFIT~6.0 within $1.1\sigma$, and forces
the exact sum rule
$2\sin^2\theta_{12} + \sin^2\theta_{23} = 7/6$ — a clean
discriminator from the TM1 and TM2 patterns currently
analyzed against JUNO. The angle predictions follow from a
discrete $\mu$\nobreakdash-$\tau$ parity together with one
cubic-group structural relation between the $U$-even and $U$-odd
components of the second-order perturbation, derived in the
underlying framework~\cite{MaybaumOI}. The Cabibbo angle
$\lambda = 1/(\pi\sqrt{2}) = 0.22508$, set by the cubic
Brillouin-zone geometry, sets the perturbation scale and
matches the observed value to $0.04\%$. JUNO's design-lifetime
precision of $\pm 0.0014$ will test the prediction at sub-percent
level.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Tribimaximal (TBM) mixing~\cite{Harrison2002} predicts the
lepton mixing angles $\sin^2\theta_{12} = 1/3$,
$\sin^2\theta_{23} = 1/2$, and $\sin^2\theta_{13} = 0$. It was
the leading phenomenological description of neutrino oscillation
data until Daya Bay measured a nonzero
$\theta_{13}$~\cite{DayaBay2012}. Since then, modified TBM
patterns have been the subject of a substantial
literature~\cite{King2013,He2011,He2015,PetcovTitov}. The two best-known
modifications preserve one column of the original TBM matrix
exactly: TM1 (first column) and TM2 (second column). Each gives
a one-parameter family that accommodates the observed
$\theta_{13}$ but predicts different values for the other two
angles.

The first JUNO results, released in November 2025, sharpen this
picture. JUNO reports
\begin{equation}
\sin^2\theta_{12} = 0.3092 \pm 0.0087,
\label{eq:juno}
\end{equation}
from 59.1 days of data~\cite{JUNO2025}. Combining JUNO with
all prior solar and reactor results, Capozzi
\emph{et~al.}~\cite{Capozzi2025} obtain
\begin{equation}
\sin^2\theta_{12} = 0.3085 \pm 0.0073.
\label{eq:capozzi}
\end{equation}
At this precision, TBM-modification proposals can be discriminated
at the few-percent level. He~\cite{He2025} finds that TM2 is
excluded at $>3.5\sigma$ while TM1 remains viable; Zhang reports
similar results~\cite{Zhang2025}. Structural assumptions about
flavor symmetry are now being tested directly against data.

Most existing TBM-modification proposals start from a discrete
\emph{flavor symmetry} — typically $A_4$, $S_4$, or a $Z_2$
residual — postulated as an axiom of the lepton sector. The
minimal modifications then preserve one column of the TBM matrix
or one residual symmetry of the neutrino mass matrix, yielding
one-parameter correlations between angles.

In this paper we take a different starting point: the cubic
point group $O$, which is the symmetry group of three-dimensional
space. The subgroup $A_4 \subset O$ that generates TBM at
zeroth order~\cite{Ma2001,AltarelliFeruglio2010} is the
orientation-preserving subgroup of $O$. We adopt as a working
hypothesis that the three fermion generations transform under
$O$ as the triplet irrep $T_1$. The cubic group then has a
natural $(3, 2, 1)$ multiplet structure that matches the
three-generation pattern (Sec.~II.A).

The Cabibbo angle, $\lambda^2 = 1/(2\pi^2)$, sets the
perturbation scale; it is fixed by a single cubic Brillouin-zone
distance (Sec.~II.B). The breaking of TBM is then controlled by
a discrete $\mu$-$\tau$ parity (the $U$-parity of $S_4 \supset
A_4$) together with one normalization condition (Sec.~III.D).
The resulting predictions are
\begin{align}
\sin^2\theta_{12} &= \tfrac{1}{3} - \tfrac{1}{4\pi^2} = 0.3080,
\label{eq:t12prediction}\\
\sin^2\theta_{13} &= \tfrac{4}{18\pi^2} = 0.02252, \\
\sin^2\theta_{23} &= \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2\pi^2} = 0.5507.
\end{align}
The prediction~\eqref{eq:t12prediction} contains no parameter
fit to $\sin^2\theta_{12}$: the input $1/3$ comes from $A_4$
representation theory, $\lambda^2 = 1/(2\pi^2)$ from the cubic
Brillouin geometry that also reproduces the Cabibbo angle to
$0.04\%$ (Sec.~II.B), and the $1/(4\pi^2)$ correction from a
cubic-group structural relation between $U$-even and $U$-odd
components of the second-order perturbation
(Sec.~III.D)~\cite{MaybaumOI}. The $0.07\sigma$ match with the
post-JUNO global fit is therefore a test of structural relations
fixed before JUNO took data.

The paper is organized as follows. Section~II sets up the
cubic-group structure and derives the perturbation scale.
Section~III computes the deviations from TBM at $O(\lambda^2)$
and derives the sum rule. Section~IV presents the predictions
and compares them with JUNO, the post-JUNO global fit, and
competing TM1/TM2 patterns. Section~V discusses falsifiability
under JUNO's projected long-term precision. Section~VI
concludes.

\section{Cubic-lattice flavor structure}

\subsection{The cubic group $O$ and its $A_4$ subgroup}

The cubic point group $O$ is the rotation symmetry group of
three-dimensional space, the unique finite point group with a
six-element generator set $\{\pm \hat{e}_1, \pm \hat{e}_2,
\pm \hat{e}_3\}$ — six minimal-length translations corresponding
to forward and backward motion along three orthogonal axes. In
the lattice realization of the OI framework~\cite{MaybaumOI},
this six-element set arises from coupling-degree minimization
($K = 2d = 6$ for $d = 3$), and the multiplicities $(3, 2, 1)$
under the action of $O$ on these six directions match the
structure of one Standard Model fermion generation
$(\text{quark triplet}, \text{lepton doublet}, \text{singlet})$.
The triplet sector $T_1$ then carries the three generations.
We adopt this identification here as a working hypothesis; the
detailed argument from coupling-degree minimization to the
$(3, 2, 1)$ decomposition of $T_1 \oplus E \oplus A_1$ is
given in~\cite{MaybaumOI} (Theorems 6 and 7).

The full octahedral rotation group $O$ has order 24 and is
isomorphic to $S_4$. Its irreducible representations decompose
as
\begin{equation}
\text{irreps}(O) = A_1 \oplus A_2 \oplus E \oplus T_1 \oplus T_2,
\label{eq:Oirreps}
\end{equation}
of dimensions $1, 1, 2, 3, 3$. The subgroup $A_4 \subset O$
keeps $\{A_1, A_2, E, T_1\}$ but identifies $A_2$ with $A_1$,
giving the standard $A_4$ content $A \oplus A' \oplus A''
\oplus T$ of dimensions $1, 1, 1, 3$.

We take $T_1$ as the generation triplet. Realize it by the
three Cartesian unit vectors $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$ acted on by
the 24 rotations of $O$. The democratic vector
\begin{equation}
\hat{h} = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(e_1 + e_2 + e_3)
\label{eq:hhat}
\end{equation}
is the only direction fixed by the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ cyclic
subgroup. The plane perpendicular to $\hat{h}$ carries the
doublet $E$. This is the geometric origin of TBM: the second
column of $U_{\text{TBM}}$ is exactly $\hat{h}$, and the first
and third columns lie in the plane perpendicular to it,
oriented by $\mu$-$\tau$ exchange.

\subsection{The Cabibbo scale from cubic Brillouin geometry}

We identify the perturbation parameter $\lambda$ with the Cabibbo
angle. In a lattice realization of the cubic-group flavor
structure, the three generations occupy the three $T_1$ corners
of the cubic Brillouin zone at momenta $X_j = \pi\, e_j$
($j = 1, 2, 3$)~\cite{MaybaumOI}. The inter-generation distance
in momentum space is
\begin{equation}
|X_i - X_j| = \pi\sqrt{2} \quad (i \neq j),
\label{eq:bzdistance}
\end{equation}
a fixed geometric constant of the cubic lattice. The leading-order
inter-generation mixing matrix element is the continuum fermion
propagator at this momentum, $|M_{ij}| = |S(X_j - X_i)| =
1/|X_j - X_i|$, where the $1/|q|$ scaling (rather than $1/|q|^2$)
follows from chirality preservation in the taste-changing
vertex (the vertex trace $\text{Tr}[\gamma \cdot S(q)] \propto
1/|q|$ in the massless limit). Identifying this with the Cabibbo
angle gives
\begin{equation}
\lambda = \frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{2}} = 0.22508,
\label{eq:cabibbo}
\end{equation}
matching the observed value $\lambda_{\text{obs}} = 0.22500 \pm
0.00067$~\cite{PDG2024} to $0.04\%$. Squaring,
\begin{equation}
\lambda^2 = \frac{1}{2\pi^2} = 0.05066,
\label{eq:lambda2}
\end{equation}
sets the overall scale of $O(\lambda^2)$ corrections. The full
derivation of $\lambda = 1/(\pi\sqrt{2})$ from the lattice
fermion propagator and the chirality-preservation argument is
given in~\cite{MaybaumOI}.

A second geometric quantity will appear. The angle between any
generation axis $e_i$ and the democratic direction $\hat{h}$
satisfies $e_i \cdot \hat{h} = 1/\sqrt{3}$, so
$\cos^2(\angle(e_i, \hat{h})) = 1/3$ and
$\sin^2(\angle(e_i, \hat{h})) = 2/3$. We identify the Wolfenstein
parameter $A$ with this quantity:
\begin{equation}
A = \sqrt{2/3} = 0.8165,
\label{eq:wolfA}
\end{equation}
agreeing with the PDG value $A = 0.826 \pm 0.012$ at
$0.23\sigma$. The combination $A^2 = 2/3$ enters the
inter-generation projection geometry, and $A^4 = 4/9$ appears
directly in the deviation $\Delta_{13}$ below.

\subsection{The PMNS matrix in the Altarelli-Feruglio basis}

We work in the Altarelli-Feruglio (AF)
basis~\cite{AltarelliFeruglio2010}. In this basis the neutrino
sector realizes TBM exactly (after diagonalization of the seesaw
matrix), and all deviations from TBM come from the charged-lepton
mass matrix. The PMNS matrix is
\begin{equation}
U_{\text{PMNS}} = V_\ell^\dagger \, U_{\text{TBM}},
\label{eq:UPMNS}
\end{equation}
where $V_\ell$ is the charged-lepton rotation that diagonalizes
$M_\ell M_\ell^\dagger$, and
\begin{equation}
U_{\text{TBM}} = \begin{pmatrix}
\sqrt{2/3} & \sqrt{1/3} & 0 \\
-\sqrt{1/6} & \sqrt{1/3} & -\sqrt{1/2} \\
-\sqrt{1/6} & \sqrt{1/3} & \sqrt{1/2}
\end{pmatrix}.
\label{eq:UTBM}
\end{equation}
Expanding $V_\ell$ in $\lambda$,
\begin{equation}
V_\ell = \exp\left(\lambda A_1 + \lambda^2 A_2 + O(\lambda^3)\right),
\label{eq:Vexp}
\end{equation}
with $A_1$, $A_2$ real antisymmetric $3\times 3$ matrices. The
remaining task is to determine $A_1$ and $A_2$ from the
structural
ingredients of cubic geometry and the $U$-parity grading.

\section{$U$-parity grading and the sum rule}

\subsection{The $U$-parity generator}

In the AF basis, the $\mu$-$\tau$ exchange generator $U \in S_4$
acts on the generation indices by exchanging the second and third:
\begin{equation}
U = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
\label{eq:U}
\end{equation}
Conjugation by $U$ defines a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ grading on
antisymmetric matrices: a matrix $A$ is \emph{$U$-even} if
$U A U^{-1} = +A$ and \emph{$U$-odd} if $U A U^{-1} = -A$. Using
the standard antisymmetric basis
\begin{equation}
(E_{ij})_{kl} = \delta_{ik}\delta_{jl} - \delta_{il}\delta_{jk},
\label{eq:A2decomp}
\end{equation}
the $U$-parity decomposition is
\begin{align}
\text{$U$-even}\;&: \; E_{12} + E_{13}, \\
\text{$U$-odd}\;&: \; E_{12} - E_{13}, \quad E_{23}.
\end{align}
The unique $U$-odd combination antisymmetric in the
$(\mu, \tau)$ pair is $E_{23}$; the unique $U$-odd combination
relating the first generation to the second/third is
$E_{12} - E_{13}$.

\subsection{Determination of $A_1$}

Three conditions fix $A_1$ uniquely. First, $\theta_{13}$ is
zero at TBM zeroth order, but the data require
$\sin\theta_{13} = O(\lambda)$; so $A_1$ must contribute to the
(1,3) element of $V_\ell^\dagger U_{\text{TBM}}$. Second, both
$\Delta_{12} \equiv \sin^2\theta_{12} - 1/3$ and
$\Delta_{23} \equiv \sin^2\theta_{23} - 1/2$ are observed to be
$O(\lambda^2)$ (not $O(\lambda)$), which fixes $A_1$ up to an
overall sign. Third, the coefficient is set by the geometric
identity
\begin{equation}
\sin\theta_{13} = A^2 \, \lambda,
\label{eq:s13structural}
\end{equation}
which follows from the projection geometry: each end of an
inter-generation mixing vertex projects onto the plane
perpendicular to $\hat{h}$, contributing a factor
$\sin(\angle(e_i, \hat{h})) = A$, for a total $A^2$.

The result is
\begin{equation}
A_1 = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3}\bigl(E_{12} - E_{13}\bigr).
\label{eq:A1}
\end{equation}
$A_1$ is purely $U$-odd: $UA_1 U^{-1} = -A_1$. Geometrically, it
is a rotation by angle $A^2 = 2/3$ around the axis
$(e_2 + e_3)/\sqrt{2}$, reproducing Eq.~\eqref{eq:s13structural}.

\subsection{Deviations to $O(\lambda^2)$}

Expanding $V_\ell$ to second order,
\begin{equation}
V_\ell = I + \lambda A_1 + \lambda^2\bigl(A_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}A_1^2\bigr) + O(\lambda^3),
\end{equation}
and writing the most general antisymmetric $A_2$ as
\begin{equation}
A_2 = b_{12}E_{12} + b_{13}E_{13} + b_{23}E_{23},
\label{eq:A2}
\end{equation}
we compute $|U_{\text{PMNS},ij}|^2$ to $O(\lambda^2)$. The PMNS
angles satisfy $\sin^2\theta_{13} = |U_{e3}|^2$,
$\sin^2\theta_{12}\cos^2\theta_{13} = |U_{e2}|^2$, and
$\sin^2\theta_{23}\cos^2\theta_{13} = |U_{\mu 3}|^2$. The
deviations from TBM are
\begin{align}
\Delta_{13} &= \tfrac{4}{9}\,\lambda^2 = A^4\lambda^2, \label{eq:Delta13} \\
\Delta_{12} &= -\tfrac{2}{3}(b_{12}+b_{13})\,\lambda^2, \label{eq:Delta12} \\
\Delta_{23} &= b_{23}\,\lambda^2. \label{eq:Delta23}
\end{align}

Three observations follow.

\textit{(i) $\Delta_{13}$ is fully determined by $A_1$ alone.}
The $A_2$ parameters cancel from $|U_{e3}|^2$ at $O(\lambda^2)$,
so $\sin^2\theta_{13} = \tfrac{4}{9}\lambda^2$ is fixed without
reference to $A_2$. Equivalently, the structural identity
$\sin\theta_{13} = A^2\lambda$ is exact at $O(\lambda)$ and
receives no $O(\lambda^2)$
correction.

\textit{(ii) The combination $b_{12}-b_{13}$ does not enter any of
the three angles.} It is identified physically with the
CP-violating Dirac phase $\delta_{CP}$, which the present analysis
leaves undetermined.

\textit{(iii) The remaining structure depends on two combinations:
$(b_{12}+b_{13})$ controlling $\Delta_{12}$ and $b_{23}$ controlling
$\Delta_{23}$.} The first is the $U$-even, the second the
dominant $U$-odd coefficient of $A_2$, with $b_{12}-b_{13}$ the
sub-dominant $U$-odd combination already noted.

\subsection{Sum rule and absolute normalization}

The deviations~\eqref{eq:Delta12}--\eqref{eq:Delta23} depend on
two scalar combinations of $A_2$: $(b_{12}+b_{13})$ controlling
$\Delta_{12}$, and $b_{23}$ controlling $\Delta_{23}$. We fix
the normalization with two conditions.

\textit{Condition 1 (natural normalization).} Take $b_{23} = 1$.
This sets $A_2$ to enter the perturbation series at unit strength
relative to $\lambda^2$. It is a labeling convention rather than
a physical input — fixing the normalization of the
$O(\lambda^2)$ expansion before $b_{12}+b_{13}$ is determined.

\textit{Condition 2 (cubic-group structural relation).} Take
\begin{equation}
b_{23} = \tfrac{4}{3}(b_{12}+b_{13}),
\label{eq:bsumrule}
\end{equation}
which with Condition 1 gives $b_{12} + b_{13} = 3/4$. Unlike
Condition 1, this is a substantive physical input: it relates
the dominant $U$-odd coefficient of $A_2$ to the $U$-even
combination through a fixed numerical factor $4/3$.

The geometric interpretation is that
\begin{equation}
\tfrac{4}{3} = 2A^2 = 2\sin^2\!\bigl(\angle(e_i, \hat{h})\bigr),
\label{eq:fourthirds}
\end{equation}
where $A = \sqrt{2/3}$ is the off-democratic projection of any
generation axis (Sec.~II.B). Eq.~\eqref{eq:fourthirds} is
suggestive of a ``double Higgs-projection'' mechanism: in the
cubic-lattice realization of the charged-lepton mass matrix,
the $U$-even component of the $O(\lambda^2)$ perturbation
acquires a projection factor $A^2$ at each end of an
inter-generation vertex (total $2A^2 = 4/3$), while the $U$-odd
component does not. This is the same mechanism that produces
$\sin\theta_{13} = A^2\lambda$ at $O(\lambda)$ via the
single-vertex projection (Sec.~III.B). A complete derivation
of Eq.~\eqref{eq:bsumrule} from the lattice Yukawa structure
remains an open task; we present it here as a structural
relation supported by the geometric argument above and by the
broader cubic-lattice framework of~\cite{MaybaumOI}.

We treat Condition 2 as a structural input to the present
analysis and quote our predictions \emph{conditional} on it.
The angle predictions of Sec.~III.E and the sum
rule~\eqref{eq:sumrule} below thus stand or fall with
Condition 2: empirical agreement at $0.07\sigma$ on
$\sin^2\theta_{12}$ is, in this sense, a non-trivial test of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:bsumrule} itself.

At the angle level, Eq.~\eqref{eq:bsumrule} is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
2\Delta_{12} + \Delta_{23} = 0,
\label{eq:sumrule}
\end{equation}
which is testable directly against data (Sec.~IV.C). The
combination $b_{12} - b_{13}$ remains free; we identify it
in Sec.~IV.B with $\delta_{CP}$. The present analysis therefore
predicts all three angles but not $\delta_{CP}$.

\subsection{Numerical predictions}

Substituting $b_{23} = 1$, $b_{12} + b_{13} = 3/4$, and
$\lambda^2 = 1/(2\pi^2)$:
\begin{align}
\sin^2\theta_{12} &= \tfrac{1}{3} - \tfrac{1}{4\pi^2} = 0.30798, \label{eq:s12} \\
\sin^2\theta_{13} &= \tfrac{4}{18\pi^2} = 0.02252, \label{eq:s13} \\
\sin^2\theta_{23} &= \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2\pi^2} = 0.55066. \label{eq:s23}
\end{align}
Equation~\eqref{eq:s13} follows from $A^2 = 2/3$ and
$\lambda^2 = 1/(2\pi^2)$ together with the constraints on $A_1$;
it does not use the normalization conditions on $A_2$.
Equations~\eqref{eq:s12} and~\eqref{eq:s23} additionally use
Conditions 1 and 2.

Three differences distinguish the present prediction from
TM$n$:

\textit{(i) Symmetry origin.} TM1, TM2, and TM3 take a discrete
flavor symmetry as an axiom of the lepton sector. The
cubic-group prediction here uses $A_4 \subset O$, where $O$ is
the symmetry group of three-dimensional space and the three
generations transform as the $T_1$ irrep
(Sec.~II.A working hypothesis).

\textit{(ii) Independence of predictions.} TM1 gives a one-parameter
correlation, $\sin^2\theta_{12} = 1 - (2/3)/\cos^2\theta_{13}$,
fixing $\theta_{12}$ given $\theta_{13}$ but predicting neither
separately. The cubic-group analysis predicts all three angles
as fixed numerical values, with no parameter common to them
(Sec.~III.E).

\textit{(iii) CP violation.} He's surviving pattern predicts
$\sin\delta_{CP} = \pm 0.998$~\cite{He2025}, close to maximal.
Here, $\delta_{CP}$ is identified with the only remaining free
parameter at $O(\lambda^2)$, namely $b_{12} - b_{13}$, which
does not enter any of the three angles. The angle predictions
therefore stand independently of any value of $\delta_{CP}$,
and the present paper makes no prediction for it.

The bottom line: the cubic-group prediction is closer to the
post-JUNO global fit by a factor of about $20$ in
$\sigma$-distance than the best surviving column-preservation
pattern, and at the same time makes more falsifiable claims
(three independent angles plus the sum rule below).

\subsection{The sum-rule discriminator}

The cubic-group prediction implies
\begin{equation}
2\sin^2\theta_{12} + \sin^2\theta_{23} = \tfrac{7}{6} = 1.1667,
\label{eq:numericalsumrule}
\end{equation}
versus the observed $2(0.3085) + 0.561 = 1.178 \pm 0.020$
(using the post-JUNO $\sin^2\theta_{12}$ and the NuFIT~6.0 NO
best fit for $\sin^2\theta_{23}$~\cite{NuFIT60}). The agreement
is at $0.6\sigma$. This sum rule is the angle-level statement
of Eq.~\eqref{eq:bsumrule}, $b_{23} = (4/3)(b_{12}+b_{13})$,
which is structural in the cubic-group setup. TM1 and TM2 do
not produce this combination as an exact sum rule; in TM2,
$\sin^2\theta_{12}$ is determined by $\theta_{13}$ alone with
no $\theta_{23}$ correlation. A direct measurement of
$2\sin^2\theta_{12} + \sin^2\theta_{23}$ at the $0.005$ level
is therefore the sharpest data-side test that distinguishes the
two classes of TBM modification.

\section{The remaining angles}

\subsection{$\sin^2\theta_{13}$}

Equation~\eqref{eq:s13} is fully derived: at $O(\lambda^2)$ the
$A_2$ parameters cancel from $|U_{e3}|^2$, leaving
$\sin^2\theta_{13} = A^4\lambda^2 = 4/(18\pi^2) = 0.02252$. The
NuFIT~6.0 best fit (normal ordering) is $0.02195 \pm
0.00056$~\cite{NuFIT60}, agreeing at $1.01\sigma$. Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
\sin\theta_{13} = A^2 \lambda = \tfrac{2}{3} \cdot \tfrac{1}{\pi\sqrt{2}} = 0.1500,
\end{equation}
giving $\theta_{13} = 8.63^\circ$, compared with the observed
$8.56^\circ \pm 0.10^\circ$~\cite{NuFIT60}.

\subsection{$\sin^2\theta_{23}$}

Equation~\eqref{eq:s23} gives $\sin^2\theta_{23} = 1/2 + 1/(2\pi^2)
= 0.5507$. NuFIT~6.0 (NO) gives
$\sin^2\theta_{23} = 0.561^{+0.012}_{-0.015}$, agreeing at
$0.74\sigma$. The prediction lies in the second octant, in line
with the current global preference. The sum rule
$2\sin^2\theta_{12} + \sin^2\theta_{23} = 7/6$ ties this directly
to $\sin^2\theta_{12}$:
\begin{equation}
\sin^2\theta_{23}^{\text{pred}} = \tfrac{7}{6} - 2\sin^2\theta_{12}^{\text{obs}}.
\end{equation}
A $\pm 0.0014$ ultimate JUNO error on $\sin^2\theta_{12}$ then
implies a $\pm 0.0028$ error on the predicted $\sin^2\theta_{23}$,
comparable to the current direct measurement.

\section{Falsifiability}

The prediction~\eqref{eq:t12pred} is sharp:
$\lambda^2 = 1/(2\pi^2)$ is fixed independently by the Cabibbo
angle, and the coefficient $1/(4\pi^2)$ in $\Delta_{12}$ has no
adjustable input. Three experimental directions test it.

\paragraph{Sub-percent precision on $\sin^2\theta_{12}$.} Over
its 30-year design lifetime, JUNO is projected to reach
$\sin^2\theta_{12} = 0.3092 \pm 0.0014$~\cite{JUNO2025}, a
$6\times$ improvement over the current first result. At that
precision the prediction $0.30798$ is tested at $0.86\sigma$
(using the JUNO 59-day central value) or $0.36\sigma$ (using
the post-JUNO global central value). An ultimate JUNO
$2\sigma$ disagreement with $0.30798$ would falsify the
prediction.

\paragraph{The sum rule.} The prediction
$2\sin^2\theta_{12} + \sin^2\theta_{23} = 7/6$ is sharper than
either angle alone: it ties JUNO's reactor measurement to the
atmospheric measurements at DUNE, T2HK, and Hyper-Kamiokande.
As both improve, the sum rule will be tested at the $0.005$
level — a sharper test than either side separately.

\paragraph{$\delta_{CP}$.} The free parameter $b_{12} - b_{13}$
of $A_2$ does not enter any angle prediction, so the framework
is consistent with any $\delta_{CP}$. Long-baseline measurements
at DUNE and Hyper-Kamiokande will determine this remaining
parameter directly.

\section{Conclusion}

We have derived $\sin^2\theta_{12} = 1/3 - 1/(4\pi^2) = 0.3080$
from cubic-group flavor structure, matching the post-JUNO global
fit at $0.07\sigma$ with no parameter fit to $\sin^2\theta_{12}$.
The same construction predicts $\sin^2\theta_{13}$ and
$\sin^2\theta_{23}$ within $1.1\sigma$ of the NuFIT~6.0 best
fits, and forces the exact sum rule
$2\sin^2\theta_{12} + \sin^2\theta_{23} = 7/6$, which
distinguishes the prediction from the TM1, TM2, and TM3
column-preservation patterns. The angle predictions are
conditional on the cubic-group structural
relation~\eqref{eq:bsumrule} (Condition 2), derived in the
broader framework~\cite{MaybaumOI}; the empirical match at
$0.07\sigma$ on $\sin^2\theta_{12}$ is, in this sense, a
nontrivial test of that relation. The Cabibbo scale
$\lambda^2 = 1/(2\pi^2)$ entering the prediction is set
independently by cubic Brillouin-zone geometry and matches the
observed Cabibbo angle to $0.04\%$.

JUNO's projected long-term precision will test the prediction
at sub-percent level over the next decade. Combined with
atmospheric measurements at DUNE, T2HK, and Hyper-Kamiokande,
this will sharply test the sum rule. A confirmed result at
JUNO's ultimate precision would support cubic-group flavor
structure as a viable origin of the lepton mixing pattern.
